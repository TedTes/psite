# Linear Relationship

## Definition

A linear relationship changes at a constant rate.

Common form:

```text
y = mx + b
```

Here:

```text
m controls the rate of change
b controls the starting offset
```

> Linear relationship = constant change per unit of input.

---

## Simple Example

Example:

```text
y = 2x + 1
```

Values:

```text
x = 0 -> y = 1
x = 1 -> y = 3
x = 2 -> y = 5
x = 3 -> y = 7
```

Each time `x` increases by `1`, `y` increases by `2`.

> Linear relationships have a fixed rate of change.

---

## Constant Rate

The important feature is the constant difference.

Example outputs:

```text
1, 3, 5, 7
```

Differences:

```text
+2, +2, +2
```

The change stays the same.

> Constant difference is the signature of linear change.

---

## Straight-Line Intuition

If a linear relationship is plotted, it forms a straight line.

Example:

```text
y = 2x + 1
```

The points line up because the rate of change is constant.

> Linear relationships are straight-line relationships.

---

## Why Linear Relationships Matter

Linear relationships are simple and useful.

They appear when:

- each step adds the same amount
- cost increases by a fixed amount per item
- distance grows at constant speed
- output changes proportionally with input

> Linear structure is the simplest form of predictable numerical change.

---

## Final Summary

- A linear relationship changes at a constant rate.
- Common form: `y = mx + b`.
- `m` controls rate of change.
- `b` controls offset.
- Linear relationships produce straight-line patterns.
- Constant difference is the key idea.
