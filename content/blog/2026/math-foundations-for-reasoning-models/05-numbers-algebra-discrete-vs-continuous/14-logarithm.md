# Logarithm

## Definition

A logarithm asks what exponent is needed to produce a value.

Example:

```text
2^3 = 8
```

So:

```text
log base 2 of 8 = 3
```

> Logarithm = exponent needed to reach a value.

---

## Logarithm As Inverse Question

Exponent question:

```text
2^3 = ?
```

Answer:

```text
8
```

Logarithm question:

```text
2^? = 8
```

Answer:

```text
3
```

> Exponents produce values; logarithms recover exponents.

---

## Base Matters

The base tells what number is being repeatedly multiplied.

Example:

```text
log base 2 of 8 = 3
```

because:

```text
2^3 = 8
```

But:

```text
log base 10 of 100 = 2
```

because:

```text
10^2 = 100
```

> The logarithm depends on the base.

---

## Simple Examples

Examples:

```text
log base 2 of 16 = 4
log base 3 of 9 = 2
log base 10 of 1000 = 3
```

Each asks:

```text
base raised to what exponent gives this value?
```

> A logarithm turns a value back into an exponent.

---

## Why Logarithms Matter

Logarithms help describe scale.

Example:

```text
2^10 = 1024
```

The value `1024` looks large.

But in base `2`, it is only exponent `10`.

> Logarithms measure how many repeated multiplications are needed.

---

## Final Summary

- A logarithm asks which exponent is needed.
- It is connected to exponentiation.
- `log base 2 of 8 = 3` because `2^3 = 8`.
- The base matters.
- Logarithms help describe scale and repeated multiplication.
