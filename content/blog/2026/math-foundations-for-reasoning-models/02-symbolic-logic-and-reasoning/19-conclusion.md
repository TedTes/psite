# Conclusion

## Definition

A conclusion is the statement an argument tries to establish.

It is the result of reasoning from premises.

Example:

```text
Premise 1: If P, then Q
Premise 2: P
Conclusion: Q
```

The conclusion is:

```text
Q
```

> Conclusion = statement reached from premises.

---

## Conclusion vs Premise

A premise is a starting point.

A conclusion is what follows from the starting points.

Example:

```text
Premise: All humans are mortal.
Premise: Socrates is human.
Conclusion: Socrates is mortal.
```

The conclusion is not assumed at the beginning.

It is what the argument is trying to support.

> Premises support; conclusion is supported.

---

## Conclusion vs Output

In computation, output is the result of applying rules to input.

In reasoning, conclusion is the result of applying inference rules to premises.

Comparison:

```text
Computation:
input -> rules -> output

Reasoning:
premises -> inference rules -> conclusion
```

The structures are related.

Both are rule-governed transformations.

> A conclusion is the reasoning-side result.

---

## A Conclusion Can Be True Or False

A conclusion is a proposition.

That means it can be true or false.

Example:

```text
Conclusion: 2 + 3 = 5
```

This is true.

Example:

```text
Conclusion: 2 + 3 = 9
```

This is false.

But whether a conclusion is true is separate from whether the argument structure is valid.

> Truth of a conclusion and validity of an argument are different questions.

---

## Unsupported Conclusion

A statement can be placed after premises without actually following from them.

Example:

```text
Premise: It is raining.
Conclusion: The moon is made of cheese.
```

The conclusion is a statement.

But it is not supported by the premise.

> A conclusion must be connected to the premises by inference.

---

## Final Summary

- A conclusion is the statement an argument tries to establish.
- Premises are starting points; conclusion is the result.
- A conclusion is like the output of reasoning.
- A conclusion can be true or false.
- A conclusion may be unsupported if it does not follow from the premises.
- Arguments are evaluated by how premises support conclusions.
