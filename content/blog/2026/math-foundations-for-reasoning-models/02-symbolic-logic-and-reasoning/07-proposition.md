# Proposition

## Definition

A proposition is a statement that can be true or false.

Example:

```text
2 + 3 = 5
```

This is a proposition because it has a truth value.

It is true.

Another example:

```text
2 + 3 = 9
```

This is also a proposition.

It is false.

> Proposition = statement with a truth value.

---

## Not Every Sentence Is A Proposition

Some sentences are not propositions.

Question:

```text
What time is it?
```

Command:

```text
Close the door.
```

Expression:

```text
x + 1
```

These are not propositions because they are not true or false by themselves.

> A proposition must be capable of truth or falsity.

---

## Proposition Symbols

Logic often uses symbols to represent propositions.

Example:

```text
P = "It is raining"
Q = "The ground is wet"
```

Then we can reason with:

```text
P
Q
P AND Q
```

The symbols let us focus on structure.

> Proposition symbols stand for full statements.

---

## Atomic And Compound Propositions

An atomic proposition is not built from smaller logical parts.

Example:

```text
P
```

A compound proposition combines propositions using logical operators.

Example:

```text
P AND Q
```

The compound statement's truth depends on the truth of its parts.

> Compound propositions are built from simpler propositions.

---

## Why Propositions Matter

Reasoning needs statements that can be evaluated.

Example:

```text
If P is true, and P implies Q, then Q is true.
```

This only works because `P` and `Q` are propositions.

They can be true or false.

> Propositions are the basic truth-bearing units of logic.

---

## Final Summary

- A proposition is a statement that can be true or false.
- Questions, commands, and incomplete expressions are not propositions.
- Proposition symbols can stand for full statements.
- Atomic propositions are basic statements.
- Compound propositions are built using logical operators.
- Propositions are the basic units used in logical reasoning.
